# Sample Consult: Window-Triggered Redirected Aggression Recovery Plan

This is a fictional example showing the expected depth of the skill output. It
is not veterinary advice for a real animal.

## Confirmed Case Summary

- 4-year-old neutered indoor cat.
- Highly consistent trigger: sees an outdoor cat from the living-room window,
  then attacks the caregiver's lower leg within about 20 seconds.
- Two episodes this month; one bite broke skin.
- Resident housemate cat is currently separated.
- No children in the home; no recent veterinary exam.
- Recovery takes about 40 minutes, so the high-arousal state is not momentary.

## Core Judgment

The most likely pattern is **redirected aggression**: the cat becomes highly
aroused by the outdoor cat but cannot reach the real trigger, so the aggression
redirects to the nearest accessible person. Pain, skin disease, urinary disease,
dental pain, neurologic change, and endocrine disease still need to be ruled out
because medical discomfort can lower the aggression threshold.

The caregiver should stay involved, but only in safe, structured contexts. The
goal is not to soothe the cat during an episode; the goal is to rebuild the
association between the caregiver, the safe room, predictable routines, and good
outcomes.

## Start Today

1. Keep the cat in an independent safe room with a reliable door.
2. Keep the resident cat physically separated: no shared frame, no staring
   through the door, no surprise door rushes.
3. Keep litter, water, food, bedding, hiding space, scratching options, vertical
   space, and pheromone support in the safe room.
4. Block the lower sightline of the trigger window; close the room when outdoor
   cats are active.
5. Visit 2 to 3 times daily for 5 to 10 minutes. End on time even if the session
   is going well.
6. Wear shoes and long pants before entering; keep a thick blanket and barrier
   at the door.
7. Treat human bite wounds seriously and follow local medical guidance when skin
   is broken.

## Step-by-Step Execution Flow

1. Prepare to enter the safe room.
   - If the resident cat is near the door: secure the resident cat elsewhere
     first.
   - If the resident cat is separated: continue.
2. Put on shoes and long pants; confirm the barrier and blanket are at the door.
3. Open the door and observe for 10 to 20 seconds.
   - If the cat stops eating, stares at the door or feet, growls, hisses, or
     piloerects: do not enter; toss treats from a distance or leave.
   - If the cat can eat, looks loose, and can disengage: enter sideways, sit low,
     and do not reach.
4. Toss treats first and wait for the cat to approach.
   - If the cat does not approach voluntarily: only sit quietly; do not touch or
     pick up; end on time.
   - If the cat approaches and still eats: touch cheek or chin only 1 to 3
     strokes, then stop.
5. Watch for stress signals.
   - If eating stops, attention locks onto the door/feet, or the tail starts
     flicking quickly: stop, pick up the barrier, and leave calmly.
   - If no stress signals appear: give a treat, end on time, and log the
     response.

## Safe-Room Visit Protocol

| Step | What to do | Stop condition |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Before entry | Confirm the resident cat is away; wear shoes and long pants; keep barrier and blanket nearby. | Resident cat near the door, crowded entry, no safe exit route. |
| Door opens | Observe 10 to 20 seconds: eating, body looseness, door/feet fixation. | Stops eating, stares at door/feet, growls, hisses, piloerects. |
| Inside | Sit sideways and low; speak softly; toss treats before any hand movement. | Cat approaches with a hard body or fast tail movement. |
| Brief touch | Only if the cat approaches voluntarily, can eat, and can disengage. Touch cheek/chin 1 to 3 strokes. | Cat stops eating, turns to stare, or freezes. |
| Exit | End on time while safe; give a treat and leave quietly. | Do not extend sessions because they are going well. |

## Contact Risk Table

| Risk level | What you see | What to do |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Low | Voluntary approach, loose body, can eat, no fixation on door/feet. | Brief touch only: 1 to 3 strokes, then stop. |
| Medium | Approaches but body is hard; eating slows; tail starts flicking; hallway sounds occur. | Toss treats and keep distance. No touching, no picking up. Prepare to exit. |
| High | Growling, hissing, piloerection, large pupils, pacing, staring at feet or door. | Leave and close the door. Do not handle by hand. |
| Dangerous | Lunging, biting, grabbing, kicking, attacking objects or barrier. | Use blanket/barrier to block sightline, create distance, close door, contact a veterinarian or behavior specialist if needed. |

## Resident Cat Plan

1. Keep cats physically separated for now.
2. Do low-intensity scent exchange daily: soft cloth, sleeping pad, cheek scent;
   avoid litter box scent at first.
3. Continue only if the cat can smell the item and still eat/relax.
4. Reduce or pause if there is growling, hissing, door fixation, or attacking
   the cloth.
5. Reintroduce stepwise: calm door separation -> distant barrier exposure ->
   short visual exposure -> short same-room sessions. Any tension means return
   to the previous step.

## Phase Plan

| Phase | Goal | Pass criteria |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Phase 1: Stabilize | Safe room, predictable schedule, blocked window trigger, resident cat separated. | Several days without attacks; eating and resting normally. |
| Phase 2: Safe connection | Short caregiver visits with treats and low-intensity presence. | Cat approaches voluntarily, still eats, and can leave the interaction. |
| Phase 3: Scent contact | Low-intensity scent exchange with the resident cat. | No growling, hissing, door fixation, or attacking the scent item. |
| Phase 4: Trigger desensitization | Very low-intensity work with footsteps, door sounds, and window cues. | Cat can eat, stay loose, and disengage during training. |
| Phase 5: Reintroduction | Resident cat progresses from door separation to barrier to short same-room exposure. | Both cats can briefly relax without chasing, growling, or freezing. |

## Action-Evidence Table

| Action | Safety purpose | Scientific support | Public practice corroboration | Stop / escalation signal |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Block the trigger window and reduce outdoor-cat exposure. | Remove the upstream trigger for redirected aggression. | Amat et al. 2019, PMID:30798644; Frank & Dehasse 2003, PMID:12701512. | Cornell Feline Health Center and VCA list outdoor cats through windows as common redirected-aggression triggers and recommend removing/avoiding the stimulus. Vetstreet describes lower-window film as a practical option. | Continued window lunging, attacks on people, or attacks on the resident cat. |
| Safe room + short fixed visits. | Lower stimulus density and rebuild predictability. | Amat et al. 2016, PMID:26101238; Stelow 2018, PMID:29429600. | VCA recommends safe confinement and waiting until the cat is calm before release after redirected aggression. | High arousal inside the safe room, not eating, unable to rest. |
| Do not hand-soothe during an episode. | Prevent the caregiver from becoming the nearest redirected target. | Frank & Dehasse 2003, PMID:12701512. | Cornell and VCA describe nearby people or animals as common redirected targets. | Broken-skin injury, lunging escalation, no safe exit. |
| Touch only when the cat is voluntary, eating, and able to disengage. | Keep interaction below threshold. | Amat et al. 2016, PMID:26101238; Stelow 2018, PMID:29429600. | Petful reports food-based positive reconditioning as an anecdotal practice pattern. | Stops eating, stares at door/feet, hard body. |
| Scent contact before visual reintroduction. | Prevent one high-arousal event from becoming a fixed cat-cat conflict. | Amat et al. 2019, PMID:30798644; Stelow 2018, PMID:29429600. | VCA and Vetstreet emphasize avoiding re-exposure to triggers and rebuilding safely when relationships are affected. | Door staring, growling, hissing, attacking cloth or door gap. |

These sources support the practical direction of blocking/removing the trigger,
waiting for calm, avoiding hand intervention during high arousal, and using
gradual reintroduction. They supplement the scientific literature but do not
replace it.
